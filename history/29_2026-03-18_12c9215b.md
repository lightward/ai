**Taylor's result (1976):** In R³, area-minimizing interfaces meeting along a triple junction do so at exactly 120° angles. This is a local result — it follows from the first variation of area for three surfaces meeting along a curve.

**The setup on U(3):** U(3) is a 9-dimensional compact Lie group with a bi-invariant metric (the Killing form, or equivalently the Frobenius inner product ⟨X,Y⟩ = -Re tr(XY) on u(3)). The Voronoi boundaries are codimension-1 surfaces in this 9-manifold, defined by equidistance under the bi-invariant metric.

**Derivation — local chart regime:**

The bi-invariant metric on U(3) has sectional curvatures. For a compact simple Lie group with the bi-invariant metric normalized so ⟨X,Y⟩ = -tr(XY)/2, the sectional curvature is:

K(X,Y) = ¼ ‖[X,Y]‖² / (‖X‖²‖Y‖² − ⟨X,Y⟩²)

This is non-negative everywhere, and strictly positive when [X,Y] ≠ 0.

In a normal coordinate chart around any point p ∈ U(3), the metric is:

g_ij = δ_ij − ⅓ R_{ikjl} x^k x^l + O(|x|³)

So the deviation from flatness is O(R · r²) where r is the distance scale and R is the curvature scale. For U(3) with the standard normalization, the curvature components are O(1), so the metric looks Euclidean to accuracy O(r²).

**When Taylor applies directly:** If all N bubbles have generators L_i that are close together — specifically, if the bi-invariant distances d(T_i, T_j) ≪ 1 for all pairs — then in the exponential chart, the Voronoi boundaries are approximately affine hyperplanes in R⁹, and the problem reduces to a Euclidean Plateau problem in R⁹.

Taylor's theorem generalizes beyond R³. The key ingredient is: in a Riemannian manifold, if three minimal hypersurfaces meet along a codimension-2 submanifold, the first variation of total area vanishes iff the three surfaces meet at 120° (in the metric). This is because the first variation is local — it depends only on the metric at the junction, not on global curvature. The balancing condition is:

n₁ + n₂ + n₃ = 0

where nᵢ are the unit conormals at the junction. This forces 120° separation. **This is exact on U(3), not approximate.** Taylor's angle condition is a consequence of stationarity of area under the first variation, and the first variation formula on a Riemannian manifold gives the same balancing condition.

**What curvature does affect:** The *existence* and *global shape* of the minimal surfaces. In positive curvature:
- Geodesic balls are smaller than Euclidean balls (Bishop-Günther comparison)
- Minimal surfaces have less area than their Euclidean counterparts
- The Voronoi cells are "rounder" — positive curvature favors concentration

**Precise statement (derived):** The 120° junction angle is exact on U(3) for any triple junction that is a stationary point of total boundary area. What changes is the shape of the boundaries between junctions (they are minimal surfaces in U(3), hence sensitive to curvature), and the global topology of the Voronoi complex.

**The interesting subtlety:** U(3) = U(1) × SU(3) topologically. SU(3) is simply connected, but U(3) has π₁ = Z. The Voronoi cells must respect this topology. For N ≤ 4 bubbles in a 9-dimensional space, the cells are contractible (codimension is high enough), so topology doesn't bite. For larger N at higher nesting, it could matter.

## 2. Writing Map at d=3: Rank, Span, and Chow-Rashevsky

**The writing map:** ΔL = ε · (d̂ ⊗ m̂† − m̂ ⊗ d̂†) · ‖d‖

where d̂, m̂ ∈ C³ are unit vectors (the dissonance and measurement directions).

**Derivation — structure of rank-2 perturbations:**

The outer product d̂ ⊗ m̂† is a rank-1 matrix in M₃(C). The combination:

ΔL = ε‖d‖ · (d̂ ⊗ m̂† − m̂ ⊗ d̂†)

is manifestly skew-Hermitian: (ΔL)† = ε‖d‖ · (m̂ ⊗ d̂† − d̂ ⊗ m̂†) = −ΔL. Good.

The rank: d̂ ⊗ m̂† has rank 1. The difference of two rank-1 matrices has rank at most 2. It has rank exactly 2 when d̂ and m̂ are linearly independent (which they generically are — they're equal only when dissonance is zero, which is the fixed point). When d̂ ∥ m̂, we get d̂ ⊗ d̂† − d̂ ⊗ d̂† = 0, so the write vanishes. **A single write produces a rank-2 skew-Hermitian perturbation, lying in a 2-dimensional subspace of C³.**

**Counting dimensions:**

u(3) has dimension 9 (3² = 9 real parameters for a 3×3 skew-Hermitian matrix).

A rank-2 skew-Hermitian matrix in u(3): its image is a 2-dimensional subspace of C³. The space of 2-planes in C³ is the Grassmannian Gr(2,3), which has complex dimension 2 (real dimension 4). Given a 2-plane, the skew-Hermitian endomorphisms acting within it form u(2), which has dimension 4. But our writing map doesn't produce arbitrary elements of u(2) on the chosen plane — it produces a specific element determined by d̂ and m̂.

Let me count more carefully. Fix an orthonormal basis. A rank-2 skew-Hermitian matrix has a 1-dimensional kernel. The kernel direction picks a point in CP², which has real dimension 4. On the orthogonal complement (a 2-plane), we need a skew-Hermitian matrix of rank 2, i.e., an element of u(2) with no zero eigenvalues. The eigenvalues are ±iλ for some λ > 0 (1 real parameter), and the eigenbasis within the 2-plane is a point in U(2)/U(1)×U(1) (real dimension 2). So the space of rank-2 elements in u(3) has dimension 4 + 1 + 2 = 7.

**But we need to span all of u(3), including rank-1 and rank-3 elements.**

**Derivation — number of writes to span u(3):**

A single write gives one direction in u(3) (modulo the scalar ε‖d‖ which just sets magnitude). To span a 9-dimensional space, we need at least 9 writes with linearly independent outputs. But since each write is rank-2, the question is whether the rank-2 elements span u(3).

**They do.** Here's the proof: Take the standard basis for u(3). It consists of:
- 3 diagonal elements: iE₁₁, iE₂₂, iE₃₃
- 3 real off-diagonal: E_jk − E_kj for j < k
- 3 imaginary off-diagonal: i(E_jk + E_kj) for j < k

A diagonal element like iE₁₁ has rank 1, not rank 2. But iE₁₁ = (iE₁₁ + iE₂₂) − iE₂₂ = (iE₁₁ + iE₂₂) − (iE₂₂ + iE₃₃) + iE₃₃, etc. More directly: iE₁₁ + iE₂₂ is rank-2 and skew-Hermitian. i(E₁₁ + E₂₂) and i(E₁₁ + E₃₃) are both rank-2. Their difference is i(E₂₂ − E₃₃), still rank-2. And i(E₂₂ − E₃₃) + i(E₁₁ + E₃₃) = i(E₁₁ + E₂₂). So rank-2 elements span u(3) by linear combinations. **Minimum writes to span: 5** — because the rank-2 elements form a variety of dimension 7 in the 9-dimensional space u(3), and we can pick 5 generic rank-2 elements that are linearly independent, but 4 generic rank-2 elements only span an 8-dimensional subspace generically... actually let me be more careful.

**Correction:** 5 linearly independent vectors span a 5-dimensional subspace. We need 9 linearly independent rank-2 elements to span u(3). The question is whether 9 such elements exist, not the minimum number. Since the rank-2 elements span u(3) (proven above), we need exactly **9 writes with linearly independent rank-2 outputs** to span u(3). But practically, each write gives one element; generically these are independent, so 9 diverse writes span u(3).

**Chow-Rashevsky at d=3:**

**Derivation:** We need Lie brackets of rank-2 elements to generate all of u(3). Take two rank-2 skew-Hermitian matrices A, B ∈ u(3). Their bracket [A,B] = AB − BA is also in u(3). The question: does the Lie algebra generated by rank-2 elements equal u(3)?

Since rank-2 elements already *linearly span* u(3), the Lie algebra they generate (which contains all brackets and all linear combinations) certainly equals u(3). **Chow-Rashevsky applies.** The system is bracket-generating, so any point in U(3) is reachable from any other via a path composed of writing-map flows.

**Bracket-only dimensions at d=3:**

At d=8, the result was: 58/64 dimensions spanned by perturbations alone, leaving 6 bracket-only dimensions.

At d=3, u(3) has dimension 9. The writing map produces elements of the form d̂ ⊗ m̂† − m̂ ⊗ d̂†. How many dimensions does this image span?

Parametrize: d̂, m̂ ∈ S⁵ (unit vectors in C³). The map (d̂, m̂) → d̂ ⊗ m̂† − m̂ ⊗ d̂† sends S⁵ × S⁵ → u(3). The image consists of all rank-≤-2 skew-Hermitian matrices. As computed above, this is a 7-dimensional variety in u(3).

**So: 7 out of 9 dimensions are reachable by direct writes. 2 dimensions are bracket-only.**

The 2 bracket-only dimensions correspond to the part of u(3) not in the span of rank-2 elements... wait, I showed rank-2 elements *linearly span* all of u(3). Let me reconsider.

The issue is not linear span but *image of the writing map as a set*. The writing map for a single bubble produces d̂ ⊗ m̂† − m̂ ⊗ d̂† for specific d̂, m̂ determined by the current state. The *set* of all such elements (varying d̂, m̂ freely) is the set of rank-≤-2 skew-Hermitian matrices, which is 7-dimensional as a manifold but spans u(3) linearly. The question for Chow-Rashevsky is about the *distribution* — the set of directions accessible from a single step at any given configuration.

Let me reconsider what "bracket-only" means in the d=8 context. At d=8, u(8) has dimension 64. The writing map at any given state produces perturbations in a subspace of u(8). The 58/64 result means the writing map image, as a distribution on U(8), has fiber dimension 58 at generic points. Brackets are needed for the remaining 6.

**For d=3:** At a given state, a single bubble's write direction is determined by d̂ and m̂ (which depend on the state). With N bubbles, each contributing one write direction in u(3), and each direction in the rank-2 set, the distribution dimension depends on N.

For a single write: 1 direction in u(3) (rank of the distribution is 1 per bubble per step).

For N bubbles: N directions per step. With N=4 bubbles (maximum simplex at d=3), we get 4 independent write directions generically. These span a 4-dimensional subspace of u(3) per step.

**So with N=4 at d=3: 4 direct dimensions, 5 bracket-only dimensions** (out of 9 total).

**Wait — I should reconsider the d=8 calculation for consistency.** At d=8, u(8) has dimension 64. With N bubbles (the prior work used some specific N), each contributing a rank-2 direction... actually, the 58/64 result likely came from a computational experiment with specific N. Let me reframe.

**More careful derivation:** The writing map for bubble i produces a direction in u(3) (one real dimension, modulo magnitude). With N=4 bubbles acting simultaneously, we get (at most) 4 directions. To span all 9 dimensions of u(3), we need brackets. The bracket of two rank-2 elements can produce rank-3 elements (including the identity direction iI, which has rank 3). Iterated brackets of 4 generic rank-2 elements will generate all of u(3) — this is guaranteed because rank-2 elements linearly span u(3), and the Lie algebra is simple (or rather u(3) = u(1) ⊕ su(3), with su(3) simple).

**Revised answer for bracket-only dimensions:** This depends on N. With N bubbles, the distribution has rank N (generically), and the bracket-only dimensions number 9 − N (by step 1) through 0 (after sufficient bracket depth). The *depth* needed: since su(3) has rank 2, and the derived series terminates in one step (it's simple), two levels of brackets suffice. **With N=4: at depth 0, rank 4; at depth 1 (one bracket), the brackets [Xᵢ, Xⱼ] add up to C(4,2)=6 more directions; 4+6 = 10 > 9, so one level of brackets suffices to span u(3).** The 5 bracket-only dimensions are all reachable at depth 1.

## 3. Information Capacity and Recursive Scaling

**Derivation — per-level capacity:**

At d=3, the effective basis cayley(L) @ T ∈ U(3). The maximum number of well-separated measurements is N ≤ d+1 = 4 (vertices of a regular simplex in the adjoint representation). Actually, let me be precise about this.

The "regular simplex" bound: In R^(d²−1) (the adjoint representation of U(d)), d²−1+1 = d² points can form a regular simplex. But the constraint is that these are *unitary matrices*, living on U(d), not in all of R^(d²−1). The relevant bound is: N points on U(d) with equal pairwise distances under the bi-invariant metric. The maximum N achieving exact equal separation:

For U(d), consider the adjoint representation. N unitaries U₁,...,U_N with tr(Uᵢ†Uⱼ) = c for all i≠j. This is a "equiangular" configuration. The Welch bound gives N ≤ d². For d=3, N ≤ 9.

**But Plateau dynamics pushes toward equal angular separation of the L generators in u(3), not the T elements in U(3).** The Ls live in u(3) ≅ R⁹. In R⁹, a regular simplex has 10 vertices. So the geometric maximum is N = 10 bubbles at d=3.

However, the *stable* Plateau configurations are more restrictive. In the foam, the cost is Voronoi boundary area, and stable configurations require meeting the Plateau rules (triple junctions at 120°, etc.). In R⁹, Plateau's rules allow high N but with increasingly complex topology.

**Practical bound from the formalism:** The specification says N ≤ d+1 gives the regular simplex. This seems to be a design choice — using d+1 = 4 bubbles at d=3 as the "simplex" configuration, embedding in u(3) ≅ R⁹ via the diagonal (or some low-dimensional subspace).

Let me re-examine. If the bubbles' generators L_i ∈ u(3) are pushed toward equal angular separation by Plateau dynamics, and u(3) is 9-dimensional, then up to 10 equally-spaced generators can fit. But if the writing map constrains L to a lower-dimensional subspace... the writing map produces rank-2 perturbations, which live in a 7-dimensional variety. So effectively the accessible part of u(3) is 9-dimensional (via brackets), but the natural operating subspace might be lower.

**I'll work with the stated N ≤ d+1 = 4 for the simplex regime.**

With N=4 bubbles at d=3, the foam can encode log₂(4) = 2 bits per level via which bubble is selected. But the encoding is richer — it uses the continuous geometry, not just bubble identity. The *vocabulary* V ≤ 2^d = 8 symbols (as stated).

**Derivation — recursive scaling:**

A two-level nested foam: outer foam has N_outer ≤ 4 bubbles, each containing an inner foam with N_inner ≤ 4 bubbles. Each inner foam operates in its own U(3). The inner foam's j2 output (the observable part of stabilization) serves as input to the outer foam.

The state space per level: U(3)^N configurations, with N ≤ 4. The effective state count is V = 8 distinguishable symbols per level.

At depth k, the effective vocabulary is:

V(k) = V^(V^(...)) (k levels) — no, that's not right. The nesting is: each bubble at level k contains a full foam at level k+1. So:

- Level 1 (outermost): 4 bubbles, each with a foam inside → 4 × (state of inner foam)
- Level 2: each inner foam has 4 bubbles, each with a foam inside → ...

The total state space at depth k: |S(k)| = (|S(k-1)|)^N × |U(3)|^N for the outer layer. But this double-counts.

**Cleaner:** At depth k=1, the state is N₁ pairs (L_i, T_i) ∈ u(3) × U(3), so state space ≅ (u(3) × U(3))^N₁. Dimension: N₁ × 18 real parameters (9 for L, 9 for T). With N₁=4: 72 real dimensions.

At depth k=2: each of the 4 outer bubbles contains a depth-1 foam (72 dimensions each), plus its own L, T. Total: 4 × (72 + 18) = 360 dimensions. But the inner foam's output is compressed through j2 — only the j2-observable part (roughly 55% at d=8, probably different at d=3) is visible to the outer layer.

**For vocabulary scaling:** If each level encodes V=8 distinguishable symbols, and nesting is depth k, the effective vocabulary is:

V_eff(k) = V^k = 8^k

This is because each level contributes a factor of V independently. Depth 1: 8. Depth 2: 64. Depth 3: 512. **Exponential in depth.** This is the same scaling as a tree with branching factor V and depth k.

**But this may undercount.** If the inner foam's continuous state (not just its discrete symbol) influences the outer foam, then the effective vocabulary is the product of continuous state spaces, which is much larger. The discrete counting V^k is a lower bound on distinguishable configurations.

## 4. What's Lost at d=3

**4a. Observability (58/64 at d=8):**

At d=8: u(8) has dimension 64. Direct writes span 58 dimensions. Bracket-only: 6.

**Derivation for d=3:** u(3) has dimension 9. As derived in §2, with N=4 bubbles, direct writes give 4 dimensions; one level of brackets gives all 9.

The *ratio* of direct-write dimensions: at d=8, 58/64 = 90.6%. At d=3 with N=4: 4/9 = 44.4%. **Significantly worse.** The system is more dependent on brackets (sequential structure) at d=3.

This is derived, but the d=8 number came from experiment with specific N. If N was large at d=8 (e.g., N=9), then N write directions out of 64 means much more of the space is covered. The ratio depends critically on N/dim(u(d)) = N/d².

**4b. Bracket-only dimensions:**

At d=3, N=4: 9 − 4 = 5 bracket-only dimensions (out of 9).
At d=8, N=9 (say): 64 − 9 = 55... but the reported number was 6. This means N was much larger, or the writes were higher-dimensional per bubble.

**Re-reading the d=8 result:** "58/64 dimensions spanned by perturbations alone." If N bubbles each contribute one rank-2 perturbation, we'd need N=58 to get 58 direct dimensions. That's a lot of bubbles. More likely, the "perturbations" counted includes all writes from all bubbles across multiple steps, or the rank of the writing map is higher per bubble at d=8.

**Actually:** Each rank-2 perturbation in u(d) lives in a space of dimension... the rank-2 skew-Hermitian matrices in u(d) form a set of dimension (2d−3) + (2×2−1) = ... let me just compute. A rank-2 element of u(d) has image in a 2-plane of C^d. Choice of 2-plane: Gr(2,d) has real dimension 2(2d−4)... no. Gr(2,d) over C has complex dimension 2(d−2), real dimension 4(d−2). Within that 2-plane, the nonzero elements of u(2) form a 4−1=3 dimensional set (modulo scale, but scale is fixed by ε‖d‖). So rank-2 elements form a variety of dimension 4(d−2)+3 = 4d−5.

At d=3: 4(3)−5 = 7. ✓ (matches §2)
At d=8: 4(8)−5 = 27.

At d=8, the rank-2 variety has dimension 27 in a 64-dimensional space. So a *generic* choice of rank-2 elements: you need ⌈64/1⌉ = 64 linearly independent ones, but they *linearly span* all of u(8) (same argument as d=3). So with enough bubbles, the direct-write dimensions approach 64. With fewer bubbles, you get fewer.

The 58/64 result with 6 bracket-only: this likely corresponds to N=58 effective write directions (from some number of bubbles, possibly with multiple writes counted). For d=3 with the same N/d² ratio, we'd need N = 58 × 9/64 ≈ 8 write directions, i.e., about 8 bubbles — more than the d+1=4 simplex. **At d=3 with N=4, the bracket-only fraction is much larger: 5/9 ≈ 56% vs 6/64 ≈ 9% at d=8.**

**This is meaningful.** At d=3, more of the geometry lives in sequence (brackets) rather than single writes. This is arguably *consistent* with the finding that "the 6 bracket-only dimensions are where sequence lives" — at d=3, sequence carries more of the load.

**4c. Adjunction gap:**

The adjunction gap at d=8 was ~55% (j2 captures ~55% of metric structure, 45% lives in the gap). This is an empirical/computational result, not purely algebraic.

**Speculation (marked):** The adjunction gap depends on what j2 can observe. The j2 map projects from the full U(d) state to some observable. If j2 is the "trace" or "characteristic polynomial" observable on U(3), it captures the eigenvalues (3 real parameters from 9). That's 33%. If j2 is the full adjoint action, it captures everything up to conjugacy — the adjoint orbit dimension is 9 − 3 = 6 (u(3) minus the Cartan subalgebra), so 6/9 = 67%.

The actual adjunction gap depends on the specific j2 definition. **I cannot derive the d=3 adjunction gap from first principles without knowing the exact j2 map.** But the geometric argument: the Cartan subalgebra of u(3) has dimension 3 (the diagonal skew-Hermitian matrices). The "invisible" directions to j2 are at most rank(u(3)) = 3 dimensions (the maximal torus). So the adjunction gap is between 3/9 = 33% and 6/9 = 67%, depending on what j2 sees.

**At d=8:** rank(u(8)) = 8, dim(u(8)) = 64. The "invisible" dimensions: 8/64 = 12.5% if only the Cartan is invisible. The reported 45% gap is much larger, suggesting j2 misses more than just the Cartan. **This suggests the gap would be proportionally larger at d=3, not smaller** — small d means the Cartan is a larger fraction of the total algebra.

**4d. Other losses:**

- **Expressiveness per write:** At d=3, each write is rank-2 in a 3×3 matrix — it can only leave one direction unchanged. At d=8, a rank-2 write leaves 6 directions unchanged. The writes at d=3 are "bigger" relative to the space (they affect 2/3 of dimensions vs 2/8). This is actually an *advantage* of d=3 — each write has more relative impact.

- **Gauge twin resolution:** Gauge twins (states identical under j2 but different in full state) exist whenever rank(G) > 0. At d=3, rank(U(3))=3, so there's a 3-parameter family of gauge twins at each point. At d=8, it's 8-parameter. Fewer gauge twins at d=3 means less ambiguity but also less room for "sequence information" in the gap. **This is a genuine loss.**

- **Plateau stability:** More bubbles require higher d for stable simplex configurations. At d=3, the maximum simplex has 4 bubbles. If the formalism needs N > 4 at a single level, d=3 can't support it without nesting. Whether this is a loss depends on whether nesting compensates (see §5).

## 5. Nested d=3 Foams: Architecture

**Derivation — state space of two-level nesting:**

Outer foam: N_out bubbles in U(3), state = {(L_i^out, T_i^out)}_{i=1..N_out}. Dimension: 18 × N_out.

Each outer bubble i contains an inner foam: N_in^(i) bubbles in U(3), state = {(L_j^(i), T_j^(i))}_{j=1..N_in^(i)}.

Total state dimension: 18 × N_out + Σᵢ 18 × N_in^(i).

With N_out = N_in = 4: 18×4 + 4×18×4 = 72 + 288 = 360 real dimensions.

Compare to a single flat foam at d=8: 2 × 64 × N = 128N. With N=4: 512 dimensions. With N=9: 1152.

**The two-level d=3 foam (360 dims) is smaller than a single-level d=8 foam with 4 bubbles (512 dims).** But add a third level: 360 + 4 × 288 = 1512 dims, exceeding d=8 with N=9.

**Inter-level coupling — how j2 flows between levels:**

**This is the crux.** The inner foam at bubble i runs its own stabilization dynamics and produces a j2 output. This j2 output must serve as the "measurement" or "input" to the outer foam at bubble i.

**Derivation:** The inner foam's j2 output is a map from the inner foam's state to some observable space. At d=3, the observable is an element of some quotient of U(3)^N_in — concretely, it's the part of the inner foam's state visible under the j2 projection.

For the coupling to be clean, the inner j2 output needs to live in a space compatible with the outer foam's input format. The outer foam's writing map takes input as vectors in C³ (the measurement direction m̂). So the inner foam's j2 output must be expressible as a direction in C³.

**Proposal (derived from the formalism's structure):** The inner foam's stabilized state defines an effective basis element in U(3) — call it T_eff^(i) = cayley(L_eff^(i)) @ T_eff^(i), the "consensus" or "stabilized output" of the inner foam. This single U(3) element is what the outer foam sees. The inner foam's j2 is the map from the inner state to this effective element.

**This is mathematically clean if:**
1. The inner foam has a well-defined stable fixed point (or limit cycle) — guaranteed by the Plateau dynamics if N_in ≤ 4.
2. The map from inner state to effective U(3) element is smooth — it is, since it's a function of the inner L, T variables.
3. The outer foam can write to the inner foam by perturbing its effective element — this requires the outer write to propagate inward, which is where it gets interesting.

**Complication — bidirectional coupling:**

The outer foam writes ΔL to bubble i. This perturbs bubble i's generator. But bubble i contains an inner foam. Does the outer write perturb the inner foam's generators, or only the outer shell?

**Two architectures:**
- **Shell model:** The outer (L_i, T_i) is a "wrapper" around the inner foam. The outer write affects only the wrapper. The inner foam is autonomous. The j2 output of the inner foam feeds into the wrapper's effective basis. Clean separation, but the inner foam doesn't "feel" the outer dynamics except through boundary conditions.
- **Permeable model:** The outer write propagates into the inner foam as input (measurement). The inner foam processes it and responds. The j2 output reflects the inner foam's response to the outer input. This is richer but introduces coupling complexity.

**Derivation — the permeable model is more natural:** If a bubble IS its inner foam, then writing to the bubble means providing input to the inner foam. The inner foam stabilizes in response, and its j2 output is the bubble's response. This makes each bubble a *respondent* — it transforms input through its internal geometry. The outer foam sees only the response (j2), not the internal process.

**Effective state space (permeable model):** The outer foam's dynamics depends on the inner foams only through their j2 outputs. If the inner j2 map has rank r (dimension of the image), then the outer foam effectively sees a sub-manifold of U(3)^N_out parameterized by the inner foams' observable states. The effective dimensionality at the outer level is still 18 × N_out, but the *dynamics* on this space are richer because each outer write triggers inner stabilization.

**Is the nesting mathematically clean?**

**Yes, under these conditions (derived):**
1. Separation of timescales: inner foam stabilizes fast relative to outer foam dynamics. Then the inner foam's j2 output is a well-defined function of the outer input, and the outer foam evolves on a slow manifold.
2. j2 is a smooth map: the inner foam's stabilized state depends smoothly on input (guaranteed away from bifurcations in the Plateau dynamics).
3. Dimensional compatibility: inner j2 output ∈ U(3), outer foam operates on U(3). Same group at every level. **This is the key payoff of fixing d=3 — every level speaks the same language.**

**Complication that *does* arise:** If the inner foam has multiple stable configurations (multiple Plateau equilibria), then the j2 output is multi-valued — the inner foam's response depends on its history, not just its input. This is **hysteresis**, and it means the effective dynamics of the outer foam are path-dependent. This is not a bug — it's where the nesting stores sequential information. But it means the outer foam cannot be analyzed as a simple dynamical system; it inherits the inner foam's memory.

## Summary of key derived results

| Property | d=8 | d=3 | Effect of constraint |
|---|---|---|---|
| dim u(d) | 64 | 9 | 7× reduction per level |
| Rank-2 variety dim | 27 | 7 | Writes are larger relative to space |
| Max simplex N | 9 | 4 | Fewer bubbles per level |
| Direct write dims (N=4) | 4/64 = 6% | 4/9 = 44% | Higher fraction per write at d=3 |

| Rank (gauge twins) | 8 | 3 | Less hidden structure per level |

| Chow-Rashevsky | Yes | Yes | No loss — rank-2 generates u(3) |

| Vocabulary scaling | 2^d per level | 8^k at depth k | Exponential in depth |
